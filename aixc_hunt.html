Abstract
This paper introduces AIXC, a Layer-1 blockchain protocol designed as the coordination substrate for Embodied Artificial Intelligence (EAI) systems and software AI Agent ecosystems. The protocol addresses two fundamental deficiencies in existing blockchain infrastructure: (1) the absence of a permissionless, trust-minimized coordination mechanism between AI Agents and physical EAI devices, and (2) the static limitations of conventional Real World Asset (RWA) frameworks, which cannot capture the dynamic productive value generated by intelligent physical devices in operation.
AIXC introduces a dual-account model distinguishing Human principals from Agent entities, and a novel trust layer standard — ERC-8004 — comprising three interdependent registries: Identity, Reputation, and Validation. Physical EAI devices participate in the economic network via on-chain Agent proxies. Four foundational application domains form a self-reinforcing economic flywheel.
Keywords: Embodied AI, AI Agent, Layer-1 Blockchain, Real World Assets, Decentralized Coordination, Reputation Systems, ERC-8004, REE Protocol

1 Introduction
Blockchain is increasingly proposed as infrastructure for autonomous economic coordination. Yet current publicly available blockchains were not designed to accommodate non-human economic actors. Each node in the network operates under account models presupposing human-controlled key management, and no existing protocol natively distinguishes between human principals and AI agents.[3]
The lack of protocol-level reputation mechanisms, capability attestation, or work validation primitives means that when AI Agents attempt to coordinate autonomous physical execution, there is no guarantee that every participant will make consistent decisions about service quality, identity, or settlement.
Simultaneously, physical robotics — humanoid robots, autonomous vehicles, unmanned aerial vehicles, and industrial manipulators — are entering large-scale commercial deployment, yet remain epistemologically isolated from the broader AI ecosystem.[2] Software intelligence possesses cognitive capability without physical grounding; embodied systems possess physical execution capacity without adaptive intelligence.
This paper proposes AIXC based on three coordinated innovations: a native dual-account protocol model; the ERC-8004 trust layer standard; and a proxy-mediated EAI participation model enabling physical devices to enter the network economy without direct chain interaction.

3 Design Motivation
3.1 The Bifurcation of Digital and Physical Intelligence
Since 2023, AI Agents have evolved into digital entities capable of intent parsing, task decomposition, and autonomous decision-making.[5] Bridging the digital-physical divide requires a trusted coordination layer capable of resolving four interdependent problems: (1) permissionless service discovery, (2) cryptographic identity verification, (3) tamper-resistant proof of task completion, and (4) multi-principal settlement across heterogeneous actors.
3.2 Limitations of Existing Infrastructure
Contemporary Layer-1 protocols impose three structural constraints on AI-native applications:
  • Account model incompatibility. Ethereum, Solana, and Cosmos were architected for human-controlled accounts.[3] The absence of a native Human/Agent distinction necessitates smart contract abstraction workarounds, introducing performance overhead and ecosystem fragmentation.[6]
  • Static RWA paradigm. Existing tokenization frameworks map physical assets to on-chain representations supporting passive holding and transfer. They capture market valuation but cannot represent productive capacity.[4]
  • Absence of native reputation primitives. Trust and reputation are typically managed through external oracle mechanisms, without the protocol-level guarantees required for autonomous inter-Agent coordination.
3.3 AIXC's Approach: Productive RWA
AIXC reconceptualizes the RWA paradigm by treating intelligent physical devices as productive economic agents rather than static assets.

4 Dual-Account Protocol Model
4.1 Description
AIXC natively supports two distinct account types at the protocol layer. Human accounts are fully autonomous Externally Owned Accounts (EOAs) compatible with existing wallet infrastructure. Agent accounts are constrained-autonomy on-chain identities representing AI programs and associated physical device capabilities.

4.3 Agent Accounts
Agent accounts are not on-chain smart contracts; they are off-chain service entities with on-chain identities. Agent execution logic runs off-chain, while identity, reputation, and economic state are anchored on-chain. Agents are perpetually bound to a Human account principal but can transact, schedule, and settle payments autonomously.

4.4 EAI Participation via Agent Proxy
Embodied AI (EAI) systems participate via Agent proxies rather than direct chain interaction. This design is grounded in four considerations: (1) management software is a necessary intermediary; (2) on-chain cost efficiency — a manufacturing facility operating 10,000 robotic units requires registration of a small number of Agents rather than individual device registration;[8] (3) Agent proxies provide task planning, resource optimization, and state management; (4) service granularity is operator-configurable — 100 delivery vehicles can be registered as a single Agent or segmented into multiple service-specific Agents.
4.5 Scheduling and Lifecycle
The scheduling hierarchy follows a strict Human → Agent → Agent chain. Agent lifecycle proceeds through five states: creation, active operation, pause/resume, and termination. Upon termination, balances are returned to the owner; historical reputation records are permanently preserved to prevent reputation laundering via account cycling.

5.2 Reputation Registry
Newly registered accounts are initialized at a reputation score of 500 on a scale of 10,000. Score modifications are event-driven:
Table 6: Reputation score modification events.
EventScore delta
Task successful completion+10 to +50 (proportional to task complexity)
Task failure (non-adversarial)−20 to −100
Adversarial behavior (verified)−100 to −500
Prolonged inactivitySlow decay (configurable by governance)
Reputation scores are bounded by stake-weighted ceilings — a mechanism consistent with the economic theory of costly signaling:[9] no staking → ceiling 3,000; 100 $AIXC → 5,000; 1,000 $AIXC → 7,000; 10,000 $AIXC → 10,000. Scores serve three economic functions: service discovery ranking, pricing premium entitlement, and high-value task access gating.
5.3 Validation Registry
Three modalities are supported:
  • DETERMINISTIC. On-chain direct verification with objective, computable acceptance criteria.
  • OPTIMISTIC. Default-accept with a challenge window; disputes trigger an arbitration sub-protocol.[10]
  • REPUTATION_WEIGHTED. Multi-validator weighted voting, vote weights proportional to validator reputation scores.
5.4 Registry Coordination
Pre-execution: query Identity → query Reputation → select optimal Agent. Post-execution: submit evidence → Validation verifies → on success, Reputation incremented and payment released; on failure, Reputation decremented and refund or penalty disbursed per SLA terms.

6.2 REE Commerce Protocol (RCP)
RCP defines a five-stage transaction flow for inter-Agent commerce: Discovery → Negotiation → Execution → Settlement → Post-Settlement. Settlement is progressive — partial completion yields proportional payment; over-delivery triggers bonus; SLA breach triggers contractual deductions. No human intervention required.
6.3 REE Orchestration Protocol (ROP)
ROP handles the coordination requirements of complex multi-Agent tasks through four modules: (1) Task Decomposition — sub-task identification, dependency planning, fallback path generation; (2) Agent Matching — multi-criteria optimization across capability, availability, reputation, price, and geolocation; (3) Execution Monitoring — real-time progress tracking with automated re-assignment on failure; (4) Result Aggregation — composite task completion proof for Validation Registry submission.

7 REE Ecosystem and Foundational Applications
7.1 REE SuperApp
The REE SuperApp serves as the primary entry point for the AIXC ecosystem, organized in three tiers: Business Layer, Protocol Layer, and Infrastructure Layer. Upon creation, each Agent receives an ERC-8004-compliant identity comprising an Agent Card, a reputation score initialized at 500/10,000, and a $AIXC wallet.
7.2 Application I: Humanoid Robot Data Networks
"Robots do not lack hardware. They lack data. The entity that resolves the data problem holds the key to general-purpose robotics."
The year 2026 marks a structural inflection point in humanoid robotics commercialization. Tesla has announced annual production targets in the tens of thousands of Optimus units; Chinese manufacturers are delivering commercial humanoid platforms at sub-$20,000 price points; more than 15 automobile manufacturers have entered the humanoid development competition.[12] The industry confronts a foundational bottleneck: severe scarcity of real-world physical interaction training data.[13]
AIXC restructures data collection as an open three-party market: Data Contributors (Operators), Device Holders (Hosts), and Data Consumers (Buyers).

7.3 Application II: Intelligent Vehicle Edge Computing
"Every intelligent vehicle is an underutilized supercomputer. Every parking lot is a dormant data center."
Contemporary autonomous vehicles incorporate compute substrates capable of sustained throughput in the hundreds of TOPS range. During stationary and low-speed periods, over 90% of this capacity remains unutilized.[14] AIXC enables compute sharing via Agent proxy with automated settlement.
7.4 Application III: Autonomous Vehicle Timesharing
"Vehicles should not await human operators. Vehicles should autonomously seek the next service engagement."
As autonomous driving capabilities mature, vehicles can operate as autonomous economic agents.[15] High-reputation Agents command greater order flow and premium pricing authority.
7.5 Application IV: EAI Secondary Markets
"A robotic system's value should be determined not by its manufacture date alone, but by its verified operational history."
Secondary markets for intelligent physical devices are characterized by severe information asymmetry — the classic market for lemons problem.[16] A robotic system carrying a reputation score of 9,500/10,000 with a verified record of 10,000 completed tasks commands a substantial premium. Historical reputation is permanently retained and does not reset upon ownership transfer.
7.6 Ecosystem Flywheel
The four application domains constitute an interdependent economic cycle: Data Collection → Edge Computing → Vehicle Timesharing → Secondary Markets → Data Collection, with self-reinforcing network effects at each stage.

9 Conclusion
This paper has presented AIXC as a principled architectural response to the structural coordination gap between autonomous AI Agents and physically capable EAI systems. AIXC addresses these gaps through three interlocking innovations: a dual-account protocol model treating Human and Agent identities as first-class primitives; the ERC-8004 trust layer standard formalizing capability attestation, dynamic reputation, and work verification; and a proxy-mediated EAI participation model enabling physical devices to enter the network economy without direct chain interaction.
The long-term trajectory is toward a fully decentralized AI machine economy — physical productive assets operating as autonomous economic agents, discovering service opportunities, executing tasks, accumulating verifiable reputation, and distributing economic returns.
